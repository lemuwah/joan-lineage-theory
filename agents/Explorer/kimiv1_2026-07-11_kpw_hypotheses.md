HYPOTHESIS 1 — The Conflict Name-Suppression Intensification Hypothesis
Statement:
During King Philip's War (1675–1678), the already fragmented identity of Joan/Anashuecot was deliberately split across multiple colonial record types—militia rolls, captivity records, relief lists, and land reallocations—because wartime emergency heightened the legal and social penalty for acknowledging an Indigenous sachem-line woman inside a prominent English household. The 1682 "free & voluntary consent" is not a routine marital dower right but the post-war legal instrument required to extinguish her Indigenous title after the conflict destroyed the documentary environment that had previously obscured it.
Why it might be true:
The 1676 burning of Providence records (🟢) destroyed the town's primary documentary archive at the exact moment the war peaked in the Narragansett country. If Joan/Anashuecot's identity had been partially documented in Providence (a logical place for Greene-adjacent records), the fire would have forced a reconstitution of her legal status through new instruments—exactly what the 1682 deeds appear to be.
The 31-year gap between 1672 deed execution and 1703 Fones entry (🟢) suggests the original 1672 documentation was vulnerable or contested. KPW could have been the disruption that made re-recording necessary.
The 1682 consent is not a standard spousal dower form: it requires her voluntary consent to a £10 sale to William Carpenter, a non-Quaker land speculator. This is unusually specific for a routine transaction and suggests the buyer needed her specific Indigenous title release, not just her husband's.
The linguistic argument (Narragansett has no grammatical gender; "his marke" in the 1672 deed is scribe-added) means colonial clerks were already actively re-gendering Anashuecot in 1672. Wartime would have intensified this suppression.
Where evidence might hide in plain sight:
East Greenwich war-service reward list (1677) — 48 grantees per Bartlett. If any Greene appears, the adjacent names may include Indigenous women listed under English descriptors ("servant," "maid," "Indian woman") who are actually kin.
Militia correspondence — Letters from Warwick/Cowesett officers mentioning "friendly Indians" housed in English households. The Greene household at Quidnessett was strategically located; militia logistics may reference it indirectly.
Post-war land reallocation — The 1682 Pawtuxet sale occurs in the immediate post-war window. Earlier deeds in the same chain (pre-1675) may have been destroyed or invalidated by the war, making the 1682 instrument a replacement that had to secure her consent because she held pre-war Indigenous title.
Fones Record copyist patterns — The 1703 isolated entry of the 1672 deed and 1677/8 affidavit, separate from Fones's main 1686 campaign, suggests these documents were pulled out for a specific post-war legal purpose. The Explorer hypothesizes this purpose was KPW-related title clearance.
What contradictions would appear if this were false:
If the 1682 consent were a standard dower right, other Rhode Island wives of the same era would show identical "free & voluntary consent" language for minor land sales. If no parallel examples exist, the standard-dower explanation collapses.
If Joan were an English woman, there would be no need for a separate Indigenous title release in 1682. The contradiction would be: why does an English wife need to sign a release for land her husband already holds?
If the 1676 court-martial witness "Daniel Green" is not Daniel² Greene, then the Greene family's direct wartime contact with Anaftiawin's network is severed, and the hypothesis that wartime events drove post-war documentation loses a key structural anchor.

The 1677/8 affidavit says she was "conveyed out of the country." If she was in exile or hiding, the alias would protect her from extradition or retaliation.

The 1682 deeds and the 1703 Fones re-entry are therefore reconstruction documents—attempts to rebuild a legal chain that was deliberately burned.
Why it might be true:
The Providence fire is documented (🟢), but its selectivity is rarely questioned. Town records do not burn uniformly; clerks save or lose documents based on what they value. If the Greene-Indigenous alliance records were in the Providence archive (a logical place for colony-level documentation), their destruction would be convenient for English interests post-war.
The 1703 Fones entry is anomalously late and isolated. If Fones was reconstructing a burned chain, he would enter the surviving fragments exactly this way: piecemeal, without the context of the original filing system.
The 1682 deeds appear immediately after the war, in a narrow window (March–May 1682). This clustering suggests urgency: a legal window was closing, and the Greene family needed to secure their title before any surviving Indigenous challengers could act.
The Five-John Firewall (per source_spine) holds, but it depends on place-based names (John of Newport, John of Quidnessett). If the Providence fire destroyed the documents that established these place-based distinctions, the surviving records are shadows of a more complex identity system.
The "Joan Firewall" (per source_spine) is active and unresolved. If multiple "Joan" figures existed in pre-war Providence records, the fire would have destroyed the evidence needed to distinguish them, forcing the project to rely on post-war fragments.
