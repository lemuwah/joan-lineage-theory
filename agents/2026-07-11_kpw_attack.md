1. Starting Question
How might Joan/Anashuecot's identity have been fragmented, displaced, or indirectly documented during King Philip's War (1675–1678), and what record types would preserve these fragments despite the 1676 Providence fire and the general colonial suppression of Indigenous women's names?
2. Hypotheses
H-KPW-1: The "Conveyed Out of the Country" Displacement Hypothesis
Claim: Anashuecot was physically removed from Narragansett territory during or immediately after King Philip's War, and the 1677/8 Absolom affidavit recording that she was "conveyed out of the country" is a post-war property-protection maneuver triggered by her displacement. The affidavit was filed to secure land rights against the chaos of war, not as a routine transaction.
If this were true, where would evidence hide in plain sight?
In the Fones Record p.6 area itself: the exact wording "conveyed out of the country" is unusual for a peacetime land transfer. If this is war-displacement language, the same document cluster may contain other affidavits using identical phrasing for other Indigenous signers removed during 1675–1678. A full-text search of the Fones Record for "conveyed out of the country" or "out of the country" across all entries would reveal whether this is a standard formulary or an anomaly tied to the war period.
What boring document might quietly reflect this?
Colonial assembly petitions for land-damage compensation filed by English settlers in 1677–1680. These petitions often list "Indians removed" or "lands vacated by Indians" as part of the damage claim. If Anashuecot's removal was involuntary, a petition from a Greene-adjacent neighbor (Wickes, Gorton, Wightman, or Carpenter) might reference an unnamed "Indian woman" or "sachem's heir" whose departure cleared land for English reallocation.
What pattern across multiple records would support it?
A cluster of 1677–1678 affidavits in the Fones Record all using "conveyed out of the country" for Indigenous signers, combined with a corresponding spike in Greene-family land acquisitions in the same parcels during 1677–1682. The 1682 home-place deed (life-estate clause) and the 1682 Pawtuxet sale (Joan's consent) would then read as post-war consolidation of rights that had been destabilized by her wartime removal.
What contradiction would appear if it were false?
If "conveyed out of the country" is standard peacetime formulary in the Fones Record, appearing in multiple non-war contexts with non-Indigenous subjects, then the displacement reading collapses. Alternatively, if Greene land acquisitions in the same zone show no spike in 1677–1682 and instead follow a steady pre-war pattern, the war-displacement hypothesis loses its structural anchor.
Testability: HIGH — requires Fones Record full-text search + Greene land-acquisition timeline overlay.
H-KPW-2: The Anaftiawin Court-Martial Hypothesis
Claim: The "Daniel Green" who testified at the 1676 court-martial involving "Anaftiawin of Narraganfett" (Hough 1858, p.181) was Daniel² Greene of Quidnessett, and "Anaftiawin" is a phonetic variant of Anashuecot recorded under war-context distortion. The court-martial placed her in a Narragansett conflict context at the exact moment the war was raging.
If this were true, where would evidence hide in plain sight?
In the complete Hough 1858 text, pp.173–191, which the project has not yet fully retrieved. The surrounding pages may contain additional testimony from the same Daniel Green, or references to other Greene-family members appearing as witnesses or defendants in the same court-martial series. Hough's index or footnotes may also cross-reference the case to other colonial records (assembly acts, council minutes, or correspondence) that name the participants more fully.
What boring document might quietly reflect this?
The colony's "Acts and Resolves" or council minute books for 1676, which record the establishment of the court-martial and its jurisdiction. These volumes often list the members of the tribunal and the witnesses called, sometimes with fuller name forms than the published transcript. A Daniel Greene of Quidnessett appearing as a sworn witness in a military tribunal would be an unusual entry for a civilian farmer, suggesting either militia service or a personal stake in the case.
What pattern across multiple records would support it?
(1) Daniel² Greene's presence in other 1676 records (militia rolls, town meeting minutes, estate inventories) showing activity inconsistent with a civilian farmer at peace. (2) Multiple phonetic variants of Anashuecot in the same court-martial cluster (Anaftiawin, Anashuecot, Anaftiawin, Anashuecote) suggesting a single scribe struggling with an unfamiliar name under wartime pressure. (3) The Hough 1858 variant aligning with the 1672 Fones Record variant in phonetic drift patterns (e.g., /ft/ ↔ /sh/ consonant confusion in colonial transcription).
What contradiction would appear if it were false?
If Daniel² Greene can be placed in non-military contexts throughout 1676 (e.g., recorded as a witness to a civilian deed in Newport or Quidnessett during the same months as the court-martial), then he cannot be the "Daniel Green" at the tribunal. If the court-martial "Daniel Green" is instead a different Daniel (e.g., from Warwick or Providence), the Anaftiawin-Anashuecot link is weakened but not destroyed—unless that other Daniel has no Greene kinship ties.
Testability: MEDIUM — requires complete Hough 1858 retrieval + Daniel² Greene 1676 activity timeline.
H-KPW-3: The Captivity-Integration-Status Hypothesis
Claim: Joan/Anashuecot entered colonial protection—voluntarily or involuntarily—during King Philip's War, and her 1682 "free & voluntary consent" legal capacity reflects a post-war status normalization that required documentary repair. Before 1682, her status may have been ambiguous (captive, refugee, ward, or unrecorded spouse); the 1682 deeds explicitly record her consent to establish clear title because her wartime status had created a cloud on the land.
If this were true, where would evidence hide in plain sight?
In the 1682 Pawtuxet sale itself: the explicit requirement for "free & voluntary consent" from Joan is unusual for a wife in a routine land sale. If her consent was legally required because her Indigenous title or ward-status created a separate interest in the land, the deed language would be more elaborate than standard spousal consent formulary. A comparison of the 1682 Joan consent clause against other 1682 spousal consents in the same volume (Records of RI & Providence Plantations, Vol. VII) would reveal whether this is anomalous.
What boring document might quietly reflect this?
Town meeting "warning out" lists or poor-relief disbursements from Warwick or East Greenwich in 1676–1678. These lists sometimes record "Indian woman in [household]" or "Indian child maintained by [name]" as relief charges. If Joan/Anashuecot was a refugee or captive integrated into the Greene household, she might appear as an unnamed charge in these lists before her status was normalized by the 1682 deeds. Alternatively, church charity logs from the period (if any survive for the Quidnessett meeting) might record donations or distributions to households with "Indian" members.
What pattern across multiple records would support it?
(1) Other Greene-family deeds from 1677–1685 showing similar elaborate consent clauses from wives, suggesting a pattern of post-war title repair. (2) Absence of any pre-1676 record naming Joan, combined with her sudden appearance in 1682 as a fully documented legal actor, suggesting a status change rather than a continuous marriage. (3) The 1677/8 "conveyed out of the country" affidavit (H-KPW-1) and the 1682 consent deeds forming a sequence: removal → return/integration → status normalization.
What contradiction would appear if it were false?
If standard 1682 spousal consent formulary in RI land records is consistently elaborate and Joan's clause is not anomalous, the captivity-integration reading is unsupported. If Joan appears in any pre-1675 record (e.g., a 1670 deed or probate inventory) as "Joan Greene" or "wife of John Greene," then her 1682 appearance is continuous, not a post-war normalization.
Testability: HIGH — requires comparative deed formulary analysis + pre-1675 Joan search.
H-KPW-4: The Post-War Land-Reallocation Heir Hypothesis
Claim: Post-war land grants and reallocations in the Warwick/Cocumscussoc area include an unnamed Indigenous heir or widow whose rights were absorbed into Greene family structures, and this person is Joan/Anashuecot. The 1677 war-service reward list (48 grantees, East Greenwich, Bartlett) and other post-war grants may mask Indigenous interests that were extinguished or transferred without explicit naming.
If this were true, where would evidence hide in plain sight?
In the Bartlett war-service volumes (Vols. 2–3) and the colony's land-grant records for 1677–1685. These volumes list grantees by name, but they rarely list the prior holders or the extinguished interests. However, the grant boundaries or adjacent parcels sometimes reference "Indian path," "Indian cornfield," or "the place where the Indians lived" as metes-and-bounds markers. If Anashuecot's pre-war land is described in these boundary clauses, her presence is structurally embedded in the grant even if she is not named.
What boring document might quietly reflect this?
Surveyor's field notes or boundary descriptions for post-war grants in the Cocumscussoc/Quidnessett area. These notes are often preserved in land-office files or town clerk volumes, separate from the formal deeds. They may contain the most detailed references to Indigenous land use because surveyors needed to describe physical features for boundary purposes, not for legal title. A note describing "the old Indian field" or "the sachem's mark tree" as a boundary point would be a direct physical anchor.
What pattern across multiple records would support it?
(1) Multiple post-war Greene-family deeds referencing the same boundary markers that include Indigenous descriptors. (2) The 48 grantees list (Bartlett) showing Greene-family members or their allies receiving contiguous parcels that together encompass a pre-war Indigenous holding. (3) A gap in the Fones Record between 1672 (Anashuecot as sachem-line heir) and 1677/8 ("conveyed out of the country") where no Anashuecot land transaction is recorded, suggesting her land was not transferred through normal deed logic but was instead absorbed through war-driven reallocation.
What contradiction would appear if it were false?
If the Greene parcels in the 1677–1685 period can be traced entirely to pre-war English purchases, militia-service grants, or inheritance from English kin—with no boundary references to Indigenous use and no contiguous absorption pattern—then the reallocation hypothesis collapses. If Anashuecot's 1672 deed land is explicitly accounted for in a separate, documented post-war sale to a non-Greene party, she is not the unnamed heir.
Testability: MEDIUM — requires Bartlett full search + Greene deed boundary overlay + Fones Record gap analysis.
H-KPW-5: The Militia Dependent Documentation Hypothesis
Claim: Greene family militia service during the war created indirect documentation of Indigenous women in their households through dependent lists, supply requisitions, or casualty reports. If John Greene Sr. or Daniel² Greene served in the Rhode Island militia during 1675–1676, the colony's military records may list their household dependents—including an Indigenous woman—for supply, quartering, or pension purposes.
If this were true, where would evidence hide in plain sight?
In Rhode Island colony military records, specifically the "Acts and Resolves" or council minutes authorizing militia levies and supply payments in 1675–1676. These records sometimes list the number of men per company and the "women and children" attached to each captain's household for quartering purposes. A Greene-headed militia unit or a Greene member in another captain's unit might have an unusually high dependent count or an explicit "Indian woman" notation.
What boring document might quietly reflect this?
Militia supply requisitions or pay vouchers. These documents are often preserved in state archives as bundles of receipts, and they sometimes list the names of individuals receiving supplies (powder, lead, food) on behalf of their households. An "Indian woman" or "servant" listed on a Greene-family voucher would be a direct but easily overlooked anchor. Alternatively, post-war pension or disability claims filed by Greene veterans may reference household composition, including unnamed dependents.
What pattern across multiple records would support it?
(1) Greene-family men appearing in 1675–1676 militia rolls (Bartlett Vol. 2–3 or colony council records). (2) A correlation between militia service dates and the absence of Joan/Anashuecot from civilian records during the same period. (3) Other militia families in the same region showing similar dependent-list anomalies, suggesting a broader pattern of Indigenous women being documented through military household records rather than civilian ones.
What contradiction would appear if it were false?
If no Greene-family member appears in 1675–1676 militia records, or if their militia service is documented but their dependent lists show only standard English household composition (wife, children, servants with English names), the hypothesis collapses. If the colony's militia records for this period do not include dependent lists at all, the hypothesis is untestable, not false—but it cannot be supported.
Testability: MEDIUM — requires militia roll search + supply voucher audit. NOTE: Providence records burned in 1676; Newport and Warwick records may survive.
H-KPW-6: The Name-Fragmentation Intensification Hypothesis
Claim: The war period accelerated the suppression of Anashuecot's Indigenous identity, causing her to appear only under English name fragments (Joan, Joane) or relational descriptors ("wife of John Greene") in all surviving records from 1676 onward, with no post-1676 Indigenous name reference. The 1672 Fones Record entry ("Anashuecot") and the 1676 Hough entry ("Anaftiawin") are the last direct Indigenous-name appearances; everything after 1676 is colonial overlay.
If this were true, where would evidence hide in plain sight?
In the Fones Record copyist patterns themselves. The 1703 Fones entry of the 1672 deed is a 31-year retrospective copy. If the original 1672 manuscript named "Anashuecot" but Fones's 1703 copy introduced "his marke" (gender-inaccurate), this could reflect a post-war copyist assumption that all signers were male, erasing the Indigenous woman's identity through retrospective gendering. A comparison of the 1672 original manuscript (if it survives) against the 1703 Fones copy would reveal whether the name suppression was original or introduced later.
What boring document might quietly reflect this?
Post-war town meeting minutes from Warwick or East Greenwich in 1677–1685. These minutes often record petitions, warnings, or disputes involving "the wife of John Greene" or "widow Greene" without personal names. If Joan/Anashuecot is the only "wife of John Greene" in this period, her repeated appearance as a relational descriptor rather than a named person would confirm the fragmentation pattern. Conversely, if multiple "Joan Greenes" appear in the same minutes, the Joan Firewall would need expansion.
What pattern across multiple records would support it?
(1) A complete absence of Indigenous-name variants (Anashuecot, Anaftiawin, Moosup, etc.) in any record dated 1677–1685. (2) A corresponding increase in "Joan/Joane" references in Greene-adjacent records during the same period. (3) The 1682 "her marke" signature reading as a colonial scribe's attempt to record a woman's mark without acknowledging her Indigenous identity—i.e., the mark is treated as an English woman's mark, not a sachem-line heir's mark.
What contradiction would appear if it were false?
If any record dated 1677–1685 names Anashuecot, Anaftiawin, or any Indigenous-name variant in connection with the Greene family or the Quidnessett area, the intensification hypothesis is falsified. If the 1672 original manuscript and the 1703 Fones copy both name her identically and accurately, the retrospective suppression hypothesis is weakened.
Testability: HIGH — requires full-name-variant index across 1677–1685 records + original manuscript comparison.

7. Notes on Documentary Environment
Providence town records burned in 1676. This is a hard limit. Any evidence that would have lived in Providence town clerk volumes is gone. Do not hypothesize around Providence-specific records.
Newport court records survived. The 1664 court record naming John Greene Sr. and the 1682 Pawtuxet sale both live in Newport-derived collections. Newport is the strongest surviving archive for this period.
Warwick records may be fragmentary. Some Warwick town records survive in private collections or later transcriptions. Their completeness is uncertain.
Fones Record is a copy, not an original. The 1703 Fones entry of the 1672 deed is a 31-year retrospective. Copyist errors, gender assumptions, and retrospective smoothing are all possible. The original 1672 manuscript may not survive.
Hough 1858 is a secondary compilation. Hough transcribed and published court-martial records from originals that may or may not survive. His transcriptions must be treated as secondary until verified against the originals.
