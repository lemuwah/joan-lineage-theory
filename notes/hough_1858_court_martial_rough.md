## Is Anawon Chief Canonchet?
No. They were two entirely different people from two different tribes with completely different fates.

Anawon (Anawan): A Wampanoag war captain, senior military strategist, and chief advisor to Metacom (King Philip). He was captured by Captain Benjamin Church at Anawan Rock in Squannakonk Swamp in late August 1676, shortly after Metacom's death.

Canonchet (Cononchet): The Grand Sachem of the Narragansett (son of Miantonomoh). He led the Narragansett resistance until his capture in April 1676 near Stonington, Connecticut.

How Were They Executed?
Decapitation, quartering, and public display of body parts were 
## standard 17th-century English judicial penalties for high treason. 
Because the English colonies viewed Indigenous leaders as subject to colonial law, surrendered or captured sachems were tried for treason or murder rather than treated as prisoners of war.

Canonchet’s Death (April 1676): Captured by Connecticut forces and allied Pequot/Mohegan warriors, Canonchet refused to make peace. He was shot by Oneco (son of Uncas) and two Pequot sachems, after which his body was decapitated and quartered, and his head was sent to the Connecticut Council at Hartford as a war trophy.

## Anawon’s Death (Autumn 1676): Capt. Benjamin Church promised Anawon his life would be spared, but while Church was away, the Plymouth officials overruled him. Anawon was beheaded in Plymouth, and his head was mounted on a pike.

## Metacom / King Philip (August 1676): Shot by John Alderman (a Pocasset warrior fighting with Benjamin Church). His body was drawn, quartered, and hung in four trees. His head was placed on a stake in Plymouth, where it remained on display for over two decades.

How Many Algonquin (Wampanoag, Narragansett, Nipmuc) People Were Executed or Killed?
While exact total counts do not exist in colonial administrative ledgers, historians estimate that out of a pre-war Southern New England Native population of

Formal Executions & Tribunals
Formal Court-Martial / Judicial Executions: Scores of named leaders and warriors were executed by firing squad or hanging in trials at Newport, Plymouth, Boston, and Hartford between August and October 1676 alone.

## The 1675 Spark: The war itself was triggered in June 1675 by the public hanging of 3 Wampanoag men (Tobias, Mattashunanamo, and Wampapaquan) accused of killing the Christian informant John Sassamon.

Mass Casualties & Overseas Slavery
Execution was only one aspect of the displacement:

Battle / Massacre Casualties: Thousands died in combat or massacres, such as the Great Swamp Fight (Dec 1675), where an estimated 300 to 600 Narragansett (mostly non-combatant women, children, and elders) were killed or burned alive in their winter fort.

## Deportation & Overseas Slavery: Over 1,000 to 1,500 captured Algonquin men, women, and children (including Metacom’s own wife, Wootonekanuske, and their 9-year-old son) were sentenced to forced servitude or shipped directly into Caribbean/Bermuda slavery, where the majority perished.

Internal Servitude: Hundreds of others (especially surrendering children) were placed into 10–20 year compulsory indentures with English families in Rhode Island, Massachusetts, and Connecticut.
