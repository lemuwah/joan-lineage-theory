The Joan Archive

Restoring Access. Preserving Primary Sources. Asking Better Questions.

This is not a genealogy website. It is an open-access research library.

Many of the records that shape this history remain scattered across archives, difficult to access, unpublished, or known only through later copies. Fires, war, colonial administration, and centuries of incomplete preservation have left gaps that continue to influence how this history is understood.

My goal is simple: Make the surviving evidence easier for everyone to see.

The Decay Crisis — Why This Matters to Everyone

History is rotting. Joan isn't lost — she's dissolving.

Before you read another word of evidence, understand the stakes: the assumption that Joan/e is "lost to history" confuses absence from databases with absence from existence. She is not unknown. She is undigitized. And the paper she was written on is actively decaying in storage facilities across three continents. This is not just Joan's problem. It's everyone's.

This was not a quiet, unrecorded corner of the world. Four colonial governments — Massachusetts Bay, Connecticut, Plymouth, and Rhode Island — actively fought over jurisdiction of this exact territory (see "The 1672 Deed Anomaly," below). Printing presses had operated in Boston and Cambridge since the 1630s. Rhode Island courts recorded transactions from the 1640s onward. The infrastructure to document a life was there, and it was heavily used by all four competing authorities at once. Joan/e was recorded into that flood of paper — twice, in 1682, in acts requiring her own legal consent.
The evidence is there. We just haven't seen it yet. This archive does not claim to know who Joan/e was. What it demonstrates is how a real lineage gets lost in plain sight: not because the record is silent, but because two forces bury it at once. Physical decay — vinegar syndrome, uncatalogued boxes, courthouse fires — destroys the paper itself. And more than 10,000 algorithmically-copied "Joan Beggarly" tree entries, all tracing back to one uncited 1904 claim, drown out what actually survives (see Contamination Index). The fabricated version is easier to find than the real one. That is the crisis this archive exists to correct.

Original land evidence volumes sit in the RI State Archives on microfilm that is turning to vinegar. Probate files from the colonial period were never fully indexed. Church records from the 1640s–1680s exist in manuscript form, some only in copies made before fires destroyed the originals. The 1676 Newport Court Martial transcript — a document that may contain the behavioral anchor for this whole question — has not been systematically digitized. [Image Permanence Institute, Rochester Institute of Technology — vinegar syndrome and redox blemish data on pre-1990 cellulose acetate microfilm; contact RI State Archives for collection-specific condition reports]

This is not a Rhode Island problem. This is a global colonial record crisis. The UK National Archives holds customs records, passenger lists, and Chancery suits that could trace John Greene's origins — most are unindexed. The Dutch National Archives in The Hague holds WIC bond records connecting Richard Smith to Manhattan — un-digitized beyond a few highlights. Massachusetts Bay court files from 1637–1645, when John Greene first appears, exist as manuscript bundles with no full-text search. Connecticut's colonial land records for the Narragansett claim period (1663–1665) are on deteriorating microfilm at the Connecticut State Library.

Every "missing" record is a not-yet-photographed record. The box that settles this question is probably on a shelf somewhere, not in a search engine — and that shelf is in a basement with humidity control that failed in 2019. We're all responsible for what happens to that shelf next. This archive treats digitization as a shared civic task, not a private research errand — see "How to Help" for concrete, low-effort ways in.

📱 Even an iPhone can save history

You do not need a $50,000 scanner. You do not need archival training. You need a phone with a camera, steady hands, and permission to photograph. A single iPhone photo of a deed page preserves that page from fire, flood, and institutional neglect forever. Every photo is a backup against decay. If you are near an archive, you are a preservation node.

🗺 Four continents, one story

Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, Connecticut, and Rhode Island all claimed Narragansett Country. Each generated petitions, deeds, court records, and boundary disputes. But the paper trail extends to London (Colonial Office records), The Hague (WIC archives), and Salisbury, England (parish registers where Surgeon John's first wife was baptized). John Greene appears in colonial records across all four jurisdictions. Joan/e appears in very few — in the fraction that has been digitized.

📦 The box is probably on a shelf — and the shelf is rusting

North Kingstown's records burned, but not everything. Probate files, uncatalogued deed copies, and private family papers from the 1670s–1690s may still exist in attics, historical societies, or unprocessed archival collections. But attics have roof leaks. Historical societies have volunteer turnover. Unprocessed collections get discarded during moves. Time is not neutral. Time is erasure.

How to Help

She's not lost. She's just not digitized yet.

Somewhere — a courthouse basement, a historical society's uncatalogued box, a probate file nobody's requested in decades — there is very likely more paper with Joan/e's name, or a name close enough to hers, sitting on a shelf. And that shelf is in a room where the humidity control failed three years ago.

This archive can't get there alone. It does not need you to believe any hypothesis. It needs more eyes on more original documents.

What we learned: The most dangerous thing in genealogy is not a wrong answer. It is a plausible answer that feels right. La Mance's "Joan Beggarly" feels right — it gives her a surname, a family, a place in the English world. It is also impossible. The honest path is harder: Joan/e Unknown. But it is the only path that leads to real discovery. And real discovery requires real documents — documents that are currently rotting in basements while we argue about them online.
The Core Question

Why does a ghost of the record have to give consent?

In 1682, a deed dividing John Greene's Quidnessett lands among his sons contains a single, extraordinary clause: the transaction requires the consent of Joan, his wife. A 30-shilling life-estate is reserved to her, with a further provision — "or to her mother if she survive."

Here is the problem: Joan/e does not exist in any record before this moment. Not in baptisms. Not in marriage registers. Not in church rolls, court testimony, town meetings, estate inventories, dower claims, or gravestones. For the entire documented span of his career — from his first appearance in colonial records (1637) to the 1682 deed — his wife is invisible to English law.

And then, suddenly, the parties to the deed require her joinder. Not as a courtesy. As a legal prerequisite to extinguish dower rights and clear title. Where does the authority of a woman who never appeared in any prior document come from? Why does a woman invisible to every English record-keeping system suddenly become indispensable to English law?

Places Joan/e should appear, but does not

  • Missing Birth / baptism record — No baptism found in England or New England. John Greene's own origins are disputed.
  • Missing Marriage record — No marriage record survives for John Greene to any woman, in England or Rhode Island.
  • Missing Children's baptisms — No baptismal records for Daniel, James, or any other child in Quidnessett or nearby churches.
  • Missing Church membership rolls — No Joan/e Greene in First Baptist (Providence), Newport, or any RI church records c.1640–1682.
  • Missing Town meeting minutes — No retrospective mention in Quidnessett / North Kingstown records.
  • Missing Court testimony — Women of her tier routinely appear as witnesses. Joan/e never does.
  • Missing Estate inventory / dower claim — If she survived John, she would have had dower rights. No claim appears.
  • Missing Gravestone / burial record — No gravestone, no burial record in North Kingstown or surrounding towns.
  • Missing Later genealogies (pre-1894) — Arnold, Austin, Huling, and other early RI historians do not mention her beyond "wife Joan/e."
  • Present 1682 home-place deed — One of two known appearances. See the 1682 Pawtuxet deed below.
  • Present 1682 Pawtuxet deed (19 May 1682) — Joan gives separate "free & voluntary consent" and signs with her mark on the sale of inherited Pawtuxet land to William Carpenter. Second independent legal act in the same year.
The quiet is extra loud. Printing presses operated in Boston and Cambridge by the 1630s. Rhode Island courts recorded by the 1640s. Multiple colonial jurisdictions — primarily Rhode Island, with claims from Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, and Connecticut — generated paper. Rhode Island produced the bulk of surviving land evidence. John Greene fought for his land at every turn: petitions, boundary disputes, colonial authority. He only submitted to the Crown after they guaranteed his land. A man this litigious, this documented, had a wife invisible to English law for ~40 years. That is not a research failure. That is a pattern.
The Authority Question

Where does her authority come from?

English common law in the 1680s did not require a wife's consent as a mere formality. A wife's joinder on a deed was a substantive legal act — it extinguished her dower rights, her claim to a life estate in the property. For this to happen, the wife had to be identifiable, reachable, and legally competent. [Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England II.129; see also genfiles.com, "Dower and Curtesy" — wife's joinder required to bar inchoate dower right]

Joan/e was all three — yet none of those qualities are documented anywhere before 1682. She did not need to be "found" in 1682. She needed to be recognized. The law reached for her, and she was there. The question is not where she was hiding. The question is: what system of authority, outside the English record, made her available to English law at exactly this moment — and why did it require her signature twice in the same year, on two separate transactions?

The "or to her mother if she survive" clause is anomalous for an English deed of this period. Within English probate practice, a bequest of thirty shillings to a wife is not inherently anomalous; such token provisions appear in cases where a widow’s primary rights were already established through dower, jointure, or prior settlement. The will alone cannot determine whether this particular wife held additional claims under English law. However, if the household operated within or adjacent to Narragansett kinship norms, the small bequest can be read differently. Narragansett inheritance custom vested substantive property rights in the maternal line, and women’s claims derived from their own kin-group rather than from their husbands’ estates. In that context, a husband’s modest testamentary gift does not necessarily indicate limited rights, but may reflect a situation in which the wife’s principal entitlements lay outside the English probate framework. [Roger Williams, A Key into the Language of America (1643); Bragdon, Native People of Southern New England, 1500–1650 (1996), pp. 195–202 — matrilineal descent and female authority in land transactions documented] The clause becomes coherent: it is not an English legal formality. It is a translation of indigenous property logic into English deed language.

The 1672 Deed Anomaly

Why does a land sale need six signatures, a counselor, and a clause about birth order?

The 1672 Fones Purchase is not a simple land transaction. It is a diplomatic document signed under conditions of legal chaos — four colonial jurisdictions claimed the same territory, and the person selling the land was doing so in defiance of at least two of them.

Competing Land Authorities — It Was Illegal to Buy This Land

  • MA Claim Massachusetts Bay asserted jurisdiction over Narragansett Country through the 1643 Atherton Company purchase and subsequent petitions. In 1663/64, Massachusetts commissioners actively prosecuted Rhode Island purchasers for "illegal" land acquisitions. John Greene's 1672 purchase from Anashuecot occurred while Massachusetts still claimed the territory.
  • CT Claim Connecticut pressed its eastern boundary to the Pawcatuck River, issuing patents that overlapped with Rhode Island grants. Connecticut's 1663 charter technically included Narragansett Country, and Connecticut magistrates periodically asserted authority there through the 1660s.
  • Plymouth Claim Plymouth Colony maintained that its 1621 patent extended westward to Narragansett Bay, and Plymouth agents interfered with Rhode Island land transactions throughout the 1650s–1660s.
  • RI Claim Rhode Island and Providence Plantations held the 1643/1663 royal charter that explicitly included Narragansett Country. Rhode Island courts recorded the 1672 deed — but their authority was contested by the other three colonies at the moment of signing.

John Greene bought this land when four governments said they owned it. The 1672 deed was recorded in Rhode Island's land evidence, but Massachusetts and Connecticut both considered such purchases void. This is not a quiet real estate transaction. It is a political act performed in a jurisdictional no-man's-land.

The legal context: In 1663/64, Massachusetts prosecuted Rhode Island men for purchasing Narragansett land. In 1665, the Crown sent commissioners (Cartwright, Maverick, and others) to settle the boundary dispute — they failed. By 1672, the jurisdictional question was still unresolved. John Greene was not merely buying land. He was choosing sides in a colonial turf war.

The Deed Itself — Six Anomalies

Transcribed from RI Land Evidence Deed #90, 1 January 1671/72. All six features are unusual for a 17th-century English colonial land deed.

1. Anashuecot — "Chiefe Sachem and heire properly"

The grantor is identified not as a private landowner but as a hereditary political authority. English deeds typically name individuals. This deed names a office — "chiefe Sachem and heire" — suggesting the transaction was understood as a treaty between polities, not a simple sale. Anashuecot is selling not just land but sovereign title.

2. Absolom — "Counsellor"

Absolom signs not as a witness but as "Counsellor" — a political office, not a personal name. His later affidavit confirms he "counseled ye Indian Sachem Mosenpp," names Tocomminon explicitly as Anashuecot's father, states Mr. Browne was left as guardian by Tocomminon, and attests that Anashuecot was "conveyed out of ye country." His presence on the deed means the transaction had advisory oversight from a recognized political counselor. English deeds do not have "counselors."

3. Six Signatures — Grantor Side

Anashuecot signs with two brothers (Wampkegge, Ompamiatt) and three sons (Seecomp, Tyecuecsha, Nammeash). Six indigenous signatories on the grantor side. Among the witnesses is Henry Greene, signing with his mark — a name requiring careful disambiguation under the Five-John Firewall (below) before any conclusions are drawn from it. English deeds of this period typically require one grantor (husband) and wife's release. Six signatures suggests collective decision-making — a council consensus, not individual property rights.

4. Six Signatures — Purchaser Side

John Greene is first-named among six purchasers: Greene, Waterman, Andrew, Tybits, Briggs, and Fones. The first-named position is significant — it indicates priority of interest or leadership in the purchase consortium. But why six purchasers for one tract? This suggests a joint-stock arrangement or syndicate, not individual acquisition.

5. The Birth-Order Addendum

The deed contains a handwritten memorandum: "Namoush being ye Eldest son by misinformation was placed last wch will not be altered." Why does birth order matter on a land sale? Under English law, it does not. Under indigenous succession logic, birth order determines political precedence. The addendum preserves the political hierarchy of the signatories even while correcting a scribal error. This is not legal boilerplate. It is a diplomatic protocol.

6. The Interlineation Clause

The memorandum also notes: "being mistaken in the name of ye third it was Interlined, Tyecuecsha." Names were being corrected during the signing ceremony. This suggests the signatories were present, reviewing the document as it was written, and insisting on accuracy. The deed was not prepared in advance by an English clerk and presented for signature. It was negotiated in real time with indigenous participants actively correcting the record.

Open contradiction — Tocomminon: Absolom's 1677/8 affidavit names Tocomminon explicitly as Anashuecot's father and states Mr. Browne was left as guardian by Tocomminon. Tocomminon is a real, distinct person in the record, not a misremembered alias for anyone else named here. This has not been reconciled with the rest of the family picture and is held open per the Contradiction Preservation Law — not smoothed, not explained away.
The cumulative anomaly: No single feature of this deed is definitive proof of anything. But taken together — a hereditary sachem selling sovereign title, a counselor signing as political officer, six indigenous signatories in birth-order precedence, six English purchasers in syndicate, and real-time negotiation corrections — the 1672 Fones Purchase reads less like an English land deed and more like a treaty between two political systems that happened to be written on English paper.

First-named purchaser, 1672 Fones Purchase. Grantor, 1682 home-place and Pawtuxet deeds. Among the most documented men in Narragansett Country land evidence. Died before 25 Sep 1685 (Bates, corroborated by the Joseph Clark deed).
