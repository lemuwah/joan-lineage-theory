The Joan Archive

Restoring Access. Preserving Primary Sources. Asking Better Questions.

This is not a genealogy website. It is an open-access research library.

Many of the records that shape this history remain scattered across archives, difficult to access, unpublished, or known only through later copies. Fires, war, colonial administration, and centuries of incomplete preservation have left gaps that continue to influence how this history is understood.

My goal is simple: Make the surviving evidence easier for everyone to see.

The Core Question

Why does a ghost of the record have to give consent?

In 1682, a deed dividing John Greene's Quidnessett lands among his sons contains a single, extraordinary clause: the transaction requires the consent of Joan, his wife. A 30-shilling life-estate is reserved to her, with a further provision — "or to her mother if she survive."

Here is the problem: Joan/e does not exist in any record before this moment. Not in baptisms. Not in marriage registers. Not in church rolls, court testimony, town meetings, estate inventories, dower claims, or gravestones. For the entire documented span of his career — from his first appearance in colonial records (1637) to the 1682 deed — his wife is invisible to English law.

And then, suddenly, the parties to the deed require her joinder. Not as a courtesy. As a legal prerequisite to extinguish dower rights and clear title. Where does the authority of a woman who never appeared in any prior document come from? Why does a woman invisible to every English record-keeping system suddenly become indispensable to English law?

Places Joan/e should appear, but does not

  • Missing Birth / baptism record — No baptism found in England or New England. John Greene's own origins are disputed.
  • Missing Marriage record — No marriage record survives for John Greene to any woman, in England or Rhode Island.
  • Missing Children's baptisms — No baptismal records for Daniel, James, or any other child in Quidnessett or nearby churches.
  • Missing Church membership rolls — No Joan/e Greene in First Baptist (Providence), Newport, or any RI church records c.1640–1682.
  • Missing Town meeting minutes — No retrospective mention in Quidnessett / North Kingstown records.
  • Missing Court testimony — Women of her tier routinely appear as witnesses. Joan/e never does.
  • Missing Estate inventory / dower claim — If she survived John, she would have had dower rights. No claim appears.
  • Missing Gravestone / burial record — No gravestone, no burial record in North Kingstown or surrounding towns.
  • Missing Later genealogies (pre-1894) — Arnold, Austin, Huling, and other early RI historians do not mention her beyond "wife Joan/e."
  • Present 1682 home-place deed — Her sole appearance. The only document that names her.
The quiet is extra loud. Printing presses operated in Boston and Cambridge by the 1630s. Rhode Island courts recorded by the 1640s. Multiple colonial jurisdictions — primarily Rhode Island, with claims from Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, and Connecticut — generated paper. Rhode Island produced the bulk of surviving land evidence. John Greene fought for his land at every turn: petitions, boundary disputes, colonial authority. He only submitted to the Crown after they guaranteed his land. A man this litigious, this documented, had a wife invisible to English law for ~40 years. That is not a research failure. That is a pattern.
The Authority Question

Where does her authority come from?

English common law in the 1680s did not require a wife's consent as a mere formality. A wife's joinder on a deed was a substantive legal act — it extinguished her dower rights, her claim to a life estate in the property. For this to happen, the wife had to be identifiable, reachable, and legally competent. [Blackstone, Commentaries on the Laws of England II.129; see also genfiles.com, "Dower and Curtesy" — wife's joinder required to bar inchoate dower right]

Joan/e was all three — yet none of those qualities are documented anywhere before 1682. She did not need to be "found" in 1682. She needed to be recognized. The law reached for her, and she was there. The question is not where she was hiding. The question is: what system of authority, outside the English record, made her available to English law at exactly this moment?

The "or to her mother if she survive" clause is anomalous for an English deed of this period. Within English probate practice, a bequest of thirty shillings to a wife is not inherently anomalous; such token provisions appear in cases where a widow’s primary rights were already established through dower, jointure, or prior settlement. The will alone cannot determine whether this particular wife held additional claims under English law. However, if the household operated within or adjacent to Narragansett kinship norms, the small bequest can be read differently. Narragansett inheritance custom vested substantive property rights in the maternal line, and women’s claims derived from their own kin-group rather than from their husbands’ estates. In that context, a husband’s modest testamentary gift does not necessarily indicate limited rights, but may reflect a situation in which the wife’s principal entitlements lay outside the English probate framework. [Roger Williams, A Key into the Language of America (1643); Bragdon, Native People of Southern New England, 1500–1650 (1996), pp. 195–202 — matrilineal descent and female authority in land transactions documented] The clause becomes coherent: it is not an English legal formality. It is a translation of indigenous property logic into English deed language.

The 1672 Deed Anomaly

Why does a land sale need six signatures, a counselor, and a clause about birth order?

The 1672 Fones Purchase is not a simple land transaction. It is a diplomatic document signed under conditions of legal chaos — four colonial jurisdictions claimed the same territory, and the person selling the land was doing so in defiance of at least two of them.

Competing Land Authorities — It Was Illegal to Buy This Land

  • MA Claim Massachusetts Bay asserted jurisdiction over Narragansett Country through the 1643 Atherton Company purchase and subsequent petitions. In 1663/64, Massachusetts commissioners actively prosecuted Rhode Island purchasers for "illegal" land acquisitions. John Greene's 1672 purchase from Anashuecot occurred while Massachusetts still claimed the territory.
  • CT Claim Connecticut pressed its eastern boundary to the Pawcatuck River, issuing patents that overlapped with Rhode Island grants. Connecticut's 1663 charter technically included Narragansett Country, and Connecticut magistrates periodically asserted authority there through the 1660s.
  • Plymouth Claim Plymouth Colony maintained that its 1621 patent extended westward to Narragansett Bay, and Plymouth agents interfered with Rhode Island land transactions throughout the 1650s–1660s.
  • RI Claim Rhode Island and Providence Plantations held the 1643/1663 royal charter that explicitly included Narragansett Country. Rhode Island courts recorded the 1672 deed — but their authority was contested by the other three colonies at the moment of signing.

John Greene bought this land when four governments said they owned it. The 1672 deed was recorded in Rhode Island's land evidence, but Massachusetts and Connecticut both considered such purchases void. This is not a quiet real estate transaction. It is a political act performed in a jurisdictional no-man's-land.

The legal context: In 1663/64, Massachusetts prosecuted Rhode Island men for purchasing Narragansett land. In 1665, the Crown sent commissioners (Cartwright, Maverick, and others) to settle the boundary dispute — they failed. By 1672, the jurisdictional question was still unresolved. John Greene was not merely buying land. He was choosing sides in a colonial turf war.

The Deed Itself — Six Anomalies

Transcribed from RI Land Evidence Deed #90, 1 January 1671/72. All six features are unusual for a 17th-century English colonial land deed.

1. Anashuecot — "Chiefe Sachem and heire properly"

The grantor is identified not as a private landowner but as a hereditary political authority. English deeds typically name individuals. This deed names a office — "chiefe Sachem and heire" — suggesting the transaction was understood as a treaty between polities, not a simple sale. Anashuecot is selling not just land but sovereign title.

2. Absolom — "Counsellor"

Absolom signs not as a witness but as "Counsellor" — a political office, not a personal name. His later affidavit confirms he "counseled ye Indian Sachem Mosenpp" and attests that Anashuecot was "conveyed out of ye country." His presence on the deed means the transaction had advisory oversight from a recognized political counselor. English deeds do not have "counselors."

3. Six Signatures — Grantor Side

Anashuecot signs with two brothers (Wampkegge, Ompamiatt) and three sons (Seecomp, Tyecuecsha, Nammeash). Six indigenous signatories on the grantor side. English deeds of this period typically require one grantor (husband) and wife's release. Six signatures suggests collective decision-making — a council consensus, not individual property rights.

4. Six Signatures — Purchaser Side

John Greene is first-named among six purchasers: Greene, Waterman, Andrew, Tybits, Briggs, and Fones. The first-named position is significant — it indicates priority of interest or leadership in the purchase consortium. But why six purchasers for one tract? This suggests a joint-stock arrangement or syndicate, not individual acquisition.

5. The Birth-Order Addendum

The deed contains a handwritten memorandum: "Namoush being ye Eldest son by misinformation was placed last wch will not be altered." Why does birth order matter on a land sale? Under English law, it does not. Under indigenous succession logic, birth order determines political precedence. The addendum preserves the political hierarchy of the signatories even while correcting a scribal error. This is not legal boilerplate. It is a diplomatic protocol.

6. The Interlineation Clause

The memorandum also notes: "being mistaken in the name of ye third it was Interlined, Tyecuecsha." Names were being corrected during the signing ceremony. This suggests the signatories were present, reviewing the document as it was written, and insisting on accuracy. The deed was not prepared in advance by an English clerk and presented for signature. It was negotiated in real time with indigenous participants actively correcting the record.

The cumulative anomaly: No single feature of this deed is definitive proof of anything. But taken together — a hereditary sachem selling sovereign title, a counselor signing as political officer, six indigenous signatories in birth-order precedence, six English purchasers in syndicate, and real-time negotiation corrections — the 1672 Fones Purchase reads less like an English land deed and more like a treaty between two political systems that happened to be written on English paper.
The Decay Crisis

History is rotting. Joan isn't lost — she's dissolving.

Original land evidence volumes sit in the RI State Archives on microfilm that is turning to vinegar. Probate files from the colonial period were never fully indexed. Church records from the 1640s–1680s exist in manuscript form, some only in copies made before fires destroyed the originals. The 1676 Newport Court Martial transcript — a document that may contain the behavioral anchor for this whole question — has not been systematically digitized. [Image Permanence Institute, Rochester Institute of Technology — vinegar syndrome and redox blemish data on pre-1990 cellulose acetate microfilm; contact RI State Archives for collection-specific condition reports]

This is not a Rhode Island problem. This is a global colonial record crisis. The UK National Archives holds customs records, passenger lists, and Chancery suits that could trace John Greene's origins — most are unindexed. The Dutch National Archives in The Hague holds WIC bond records connecting Richard Smith to Manhattan — un-digitized beyond a few highlights. Massachusetts Bay court files from 1637–1645, when John Greene first appears, exist as manuscript bundles with no full-text search. Connecticut's colonial land records for the Narragansett claim period (1663–1665) are on deteriorating microfilm at the Connecticut State Library.

Every "missing" record is a not-yet-photographed record. The box that settles this question is probably on a shelf somewhere, not in a search engine — and that shelf is in a basement with humidity control that failed in 2019.

📱 Even an iPhone can save history

You do not need a $50,000 scanner. You do not need archival training. You need a phone with a camera, steady hands, and permission to photograph. A single iPhone photo of a deed page preserves that page from fire, flood, and institutional neglect forever. Every photo is a backup against decay. If you are near an archive, you are a preservation node.

🗺 Four continents, one story

Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, Connecticut, and Rhode Island all claimed Narragansett Country. Each generated petitions, deeds, court records, and boundary disputes. But the paper trail extends to London (Colonial Office records), The Hague (WIC archives), and Salisbury, England (parish registers where Surgeon John's first wife was baptized). John Greene appears in colonial records across all four jurisdictions. Joan/e appears in none — in the fraction that has been digitized.

📦 The box is probably on a shelf — and the shelf is rusting

North Kingstown's records burned, but not everything. Probate files, uncatalogued deed copies, and private family papers from the 1670s–1690s may still exist in attics, historical societies, or unprocessed archival collections. But attics have roof leaks. Historical societies have volunteer turnover. Unprocessed collections get discarded during moves. Time is not neutral. Time is erasure.

Competing Hypotheses

Stress Test: Which explanation survives scrutiny?

Every hypothesis must explain three things: geographic continuity (why this specific land?), archival silence (why no English records?), and the 1682 life-estate clause (why the anomalous language?). All plausible alternatives are included — not just the preferred hypothesis vs. a straw man.

PARTIAL — A widow might retain dower rights from a prior marriage, but the "or to her mother" clause remains anomalous. No English widow's dower extends to her mother.
E. 1682 Deed Interpolation / Forgery NO — The deed is recorded in RI Land Evidence Vol. IV with consistent witness clustering. No evidence of tampering. The 1685 deed (Edward Greene) and other family conveyances cross-reference the 1682 division without contradiction. NO — Forgery would require motive (who gains by inventing Joan?) and opportunity (all witnesses complicit?). No beneficiary of a forged Joan is visible. The clause actually reduces the sons' inheritance by reserving a life estate. NO — A forger would use standard English legal language. The anomalous "or to her mother" clause is the hardest thing to explain as a forgery — it is too weird to be fake.
Methodological note: Hypothesis B (Kinship Union) is the only explanation that survives all three stress tests without requiring special pleading. Hypotheses C and D fail on the life-estate clause. Hypothesis E fails on documentary integrity. Hypothesis A fails on all three counts. This does not prove B. It means B is the only hypothesis currently consistent with all known evidence. A single new document could overturn it.

🗺 Geographic Continuity — "Land as Witness"

The 1672 Fones Purchase deed (Anashuecot) and the 1682 home-place deed (Joan Greene) both concern land in the Quidnessett/East Greenwich corridor. John Greene is first-named purchaser in 1672; grantor in 1682. The land boundaries are contiguous. The question is whether the property rights derive from the same source.

Forensic test: Compare boundary descriptions. If "bounded by the river" or "near the path of Womphogg" appear in both deeds, the land identity strengthens the personal identity hypothesis.

⏳ Temporal Alignment — "The Presence-Absence Shift"

Anashuecot appears in 1672 — the last confirmed primary source. Joan Greene appears in 1682 — the first confirmed primary source. The 10-year gap (1672–1682) coincides with the most catastrophic period in Narragansett history: the Great Swamp Massacre (Dec 1675) [Increase Mather, A Brief History of the War with the Indians in New-England (1676); Benjamin Church, Entertaining Passages Relating to Philip's War (1716), p. 13 — assault on Narragansett fort, 19 Dec 1675], Metacom's death (Aug 12, 1676) [Benjamin Church, Entertaining Passages Relating to Philip's War (1716); Hubbard, The History of the Indian Wars (1677), Drake ed. (1865), vol. I, pp. 120–122 — Metacom shot by John Alderman in Miery Swamp near Mount Hope, Bristol, RI], and the mass enslavement/deportation of 500+ Narragansett [Margaret Ellen Newell, Brethren by Nature: New England Indians, Colonists, and the Origins of American Slavery (2015), pp. 142–143; see also Easton & Hough, Narrative of the Causes Which Led to Philip's Indian War (1858), p. 29n2 — Narragansett shipped to Barbados, Bermuda, and Mediterranean ports post-1676].

The shift: Post-1676, surviving Narragansett individuals in colonial documents were increasingly recorded by English names rather than Narragansett names. "Anashuecot" (1672) → "Joan" (1682) is consistent with documented post-war anglicization patterns.

⚖ Behavioral Anomaly — "The Kinship Strategy"

At the 1676 Newport Court Martial, John Greene denied charges while his son Daniel testified for the English prosecution. A father-son split in the same proceeding is atypical for colonial court records. If Daniel was the child of an English mother, his testimony against his father is difficult to explain. If Daniel was the child of a Narragansett mother, his testimony for the English against his father's interests becomes a survival strategy: aligning with the winning side to protect matrilineal land rights. Alternative explanations exist: estrangement, coercion, bribery, or genuine belief in his father's guilt. The kinship hypothesis is not the only possible account of this behavior.

📜 Archival Signature — "The Produced Absence"

Joan/e's invisibility is not random. It is structured. She is absent from every English record-keeping system (church, court, probate, gravestone) and present only at the two moments when her land rights intersected English legal process: the 1672 purchase (as Anashuecot, the seller) and the 1682 division (as Joan, the wife whose consent was required). This is exactly the documentary footprint a Narragansett-English kinship union would be expected to leave.

Comparator: Sarah Stanton/Tibbitts, Mary Tibbitts/Greene, and Elizabeth Wickes all appear in church rolls, court testimony, and estate documents. Joan/e appears in none. The difference is not research failure. It is a different legal category.

The "Female Inheritance Window" (1676–1682)

With male sachems dead or enslaved, English purchasers strategically sought out indigenous women (Sunksquaws) as grantors, recognizing that their signatures made titles more defensible in English courts.

This was a regional pattern, not unique to John Greene. After King Philip's War, English settlers across southern New England sought signatures from indigenous women to secure land titles that male sachems could no longer validate. Quaiapen (the Old Queen) and other high-status Narragansett women appear in post-1676 deeds as grantors. The 1682 home-place deed requiring Joan's joinder fits this documented pattern — whether Joan was Anashuecot or not, the structure of requiring a female indigenous signatory in 1682 is consistent with the broader "Female Inheritance Window."

Joan's appearance in 1682 is one instance within this window. It functions as a Title Confirmation — the English needed "Joan" to sign so they could legally own what indigenous authorities had previously possessed. In Narragansett culture, women held significant land rights. If Joan/Anashuecot was a Sunksquaw, it explains why the English had to have her signature to make their land "legal" in the eyes of the Crown. [Newell, Brethren by Nature (2015), pp. 142–143 — post-war indigenous women as grantors; Bragdon, Native People of Southern New England (1996), pp. 195–202 — female authority in land transactions]

The "Canonchet Proximity" — the documented pattern of high-status Narragansett women being recorded under English names immediately following the April 1676 execution of Canonchet (Nanuntenoo) [William Hubbard, The History of the Indian Wars in New England (1677), reprinted Drake ed. (1865), vol. I, pp. 120–122; Increase Mather, Relation of the Troubles (1676) — captured 2 Apr 1676, executed 3 Apr 1676 at Stonington, CT by Pequot/Mohegan/Niantic executioners] — is historically consistent: the execution of Canonchet (Nanuntenoo) in April 1676 was the most significant political event in the region. A high-status woman being "hidden" as "Joan" immediately following that event aligns with the documented "Great Migration" and "Erasure" periods of Narragansett history.

The 4 Laws

The operating principles of this archive

These four laws exist to prevent the most common errors in genealogical research: narrative smoothing, circular citation, premature exclusion, and algorithmic contamination. They are not decorative. They are enforced.

1 No Smoothing

Do not fill gaps with plausible stories. If the record is silent, say so. If two sources conflict, present both. The most dangerous thing in genealogy is not a wrong answer — it is a plausible answer that feels right. La Mance's "Joan Beggarly" feels right. It is also impossible. No smoothing means no comfort narratives. Enforcement: Every claim without a primary source gets a [Easton & Hough, Narrative of the Causes Which Led to Philip's Indian War (1858), pp. 29n2, 94–95 — Hough publishes verbatim court-martial records from Newport, Aug 1676; John Greene and Daniel Greene both named] flag. Every hypothesis table includes all alternatives, not just the preferred one.

2 La Mance Sieve

La Mance (1904) is the primary contamination vector for this family. Every claim in La Mance must be verified against primary sources before acceptance. The default assumption is error, not authority. No citation? No acceptance. Chronologically impossible? Rejected. This sieve applies to everything downstream of La Mance too — Rixford (1932), algorithmic trees, and compiled genealogies that cite him. Enforcement: La Mance claims are tagged Tier 4 (INVALIDATED) unless independently verified. No exceptions.

3 No Early Exclusion

Do not dismiss hypotheses because they are uncomfortable or unconventional. The Narragansett hypothesis for Joan/e is not proven, but it is not disproven either. It remains in the record as a working hypothesis with its evidence weighed honestly. Early exclusion is how plausible answers get buried before they are tested. Enforcement: The hypothesis table includes the "forgery" alternative (Hypothesis E) despite its low probability, because it is logically possible and must be eliminated, not ignored.

4 No Algorithmic Contamination

Ancestry, FamilySearch, MyHeritage, and YourRoots algorithmically merge profiles, propagate errors, and create circular citations. A tree with 10,000 matches is not 10,000 independent confirmations. It is one error copied 10,000 times. This archive does not accept algorithmic trees as sources. Ever. Enforcement: No algorithmic tree citations appear in the Primary Sources table. The Contamination Index documents how algorithmic propagation works so visitors can recognize it in their own research.

How to Help

She's not lost. She's just not digitized yet.

Somewhere — a courthouse basement, a historical society's uncatalogued box, a probate file nobody's requested in decades — there is very likely more paper with Joan/e's name, or a name close enough to hers, sitting on a shelf. And that shelf is in a room where the humidity control failed three years ago.

This archive can't get there alone. It does not need you to believe any hypothesis. It needs more eyes on more original documents.

RI State Archives, RIHS, Kings County probate, North Kingstown land evidence. Use a document scanning app (Adobe Scan, Microsoft Lens, or native iOS/Android scan) for flat correction. Upload to the GitHub repository or email to the archive maintainer. One photo = one page saved from fire, flood, and institutional neglect.

Several images still need careful transcription or second opinions on hard-to-read marks. The 1676 court martial verbatim transcript is the highest priority. The phonetic audit table has four incomplete rows waiting for Hubbard, Huling, Austin, and La Mance variant entries.

👤 If you know a name

Sarah Tibbitts, Mary Tibbitts/Greene, Elizabeth Wickes, and any woman connected to the Fones household are real, findable comparison points. What you know could sharpen this whole picture. The unidentified female resident of the Fones household is a specific target — who was she?

🏛 If you're an institution

This project exists to make primary sources easier to reach, not to replace archives. Digitization capacity, cataloguing expertise, or pointers to unprocessed collections are exactly what is needed. If you have uncatalogued 17th-century land evidence, probate files, or church records touching Narragansett Country, this archive wants to know they exist.

🌍 If you're anywhere on Earth

Share this archive. The algorithmic contamination problem (Trap 3) spreads because people don't know there is an alternative. Every person who learns to check primary sources before accepting an Ancestry hint is a node in the resistance. Preservation is not just about old paper. It is about stopping the replication of error.
