Why Joan/e?

Who was she?

We know her name appears once: the 1682 division deed. Every other record — baptisms, marriages, church rolls, court testimony, dower claims, gravestones — is silent. For roughly thirty years, a documented man's wife is a ghost. That is not a research failure. That is a pattern.

Why the silence?

Printing presses operated in Boston by the 1630s. Rhode Island courts recorded by the 1640s. John Greene fought for his land at every turn — petitions, boundary disputes, colonial authority. He only submitted to the Crown after they guaranteed his land. A man this litigious, this papered, had a wife invisible to English law. The quiet is extra loud.

What if she wasn't English?

The Narragansett hypothesis: Joan/e may be connected to the sachem lineage, possibly identifiable with Anashuecot named in the 1672 Fones Purchase deed. If she lived between two legal worlds, English records would not recognize her name, her marriages, or her children. Her single appearance in 1682 — when English law required a wife's name — would be the exception that proves the rule.

Can you help find her?

This archive is open access because the evidence is scattered, unpublished, or known only through copies made before fires and war destroyed originals. If you have access to archives, family papers, or local records not listed here, reach out. The box waiting to be found might be in your attic.

Why the firewall matters

La Mance (1904) assigned Joan/e the surname "Beggarly" and identified her as the wife of Surgeon John Greene of Warwick. This is chronologically impossible on two independent grounds: (1) Alice Beggarly, Surgeon John's documented wife, died in January 1643/44 — 38 years before Joan/e's 1682 appearance; (2) Surgeon John's documented geographic sphere was Shawomet/Warwick, not Quidnessett/Narragansett, while Joan/e appears exclusively in Quidnessett documentation. La Mance provides no citations, manuscript references, or archival basis for the "Joan Beggarly" claim. Status: [INVALIDATED]

Where is Joan/e? The ~40-year silence

Places Joan/e should appear, but doesn't

    The quiet is extra loud. Printing presses were operating in Boston and Cambridge by the 1630s. Rhode Island's courts were recording by the 1640s. Land disputes generated paper trails throughout Narragansett Country. John Greene fought for his land at every turn. He only submitted to Crown authority (Rhode Island) after they let him keep his land. A man this litigious, this documented, had a wife who is invisible for ~40 years. That is not normal. That is a pattern.

    The Narragansett hypothesis (five-lane convergence)

    One working theory: Joan/e may be connected to the Narragansett sachem lineage, possibly identifiable with Anashuecot (named in the 1672 Fones Purchase deed alongside John Greene as first-named purchaser). If she was Narragansett, English legal records would not have recognized her name, her marriages, or her children in the same way. Her single appearance in 1682 — when English law finally required a wife's name on a land division — would be consistent with a woman who lived between two legal worlds.

    Overall confidence: ~65-73% (adjusted -15% from working estimates). Five independent lanes: Geographic (~60%), Phonetic (~55%), Status/Anglicization (~60%), Archival Silence (~55%), Behavioral (~75%). No single lane is conclusive. Their convergence supports the hypothesis under parsimony.

    Working hypothesis Unproven

    The "or to her mother if she survive" clause

    The 1682 deed reserves a 30-shilling life-estate to Joan/e, extending to her mother if Joan/e predeceases. If Joan/e were the daughter of Alice Beggarly (d. January 1643/44), this clause would be legally meaningless — Alice had been dead for 38 years. The clause is:

    • (a) Anomalous for an English deed of this period
    • (b) Inexplicable if Joan/e's mother was Alice Beggarly
    • (c) Fully coherent under Narragansett matrilineal succession: property rights derived from the Awas- lineage, reverting to the senior matrilineal figure

    The struggle to get to the truth

    This archive exists because the path to honest genealogy is littered with traps. Here is how we fell into them, climbed out, and what we learned.

    Trap 1: The La Mance poison

    La Mance (1904) assigned Joan/e the surname "Beggarly" and identified her as the wife of Surgeon John Greene of Warwick. This claim propagated through Rixford (1932) and into every major genealogy database. The problem: it is chronologically impossible. Alice Beggarly, Surgeon John's documented wife, died in January 1643/44 — 38 years before Joan/e's 1682 appearance. La Mance provides no citations, no manuscript references, no archival basis. We spent months untangling this.

    Tier 4 — INVALIDATED

    Trap 2: The two-John conflation

    Genealogical databases algorithmically merge multiple men named John Greene into a single profile. Surgeon John (Warwick, d.1659), Quidnessett John (our subject), Newark John (New Jersey, 1666), and Stonewall John (Narragansett, d.1676) become one person. Wives are swapped. Children are reassigned. Land records from four men attach to one profile. The 4-John firewall took weeks to build from primary sources.

    Resolved — 4-John Firewall VERIFIED

    Trap 3: The "Joan Beggarly" circular citation

    Ancestry trees cite FamilySearch. FamilySearch cites Ancestry. Both cite La Mance. La Mance cites nothing. The circle is complete and empty. We traced the propagation through Rixford (1932) and subsequent compilations. No primary source exists for "Joan Beggarly." The name is a fiction that became a fact through repetition.

    Tier 4 — INVALIDATED

    Trap 4: The 1635 passenger list

    La Mance connected Quidnessett John to a John Greene on the 1635 passenger list. Huling (1884) stated explicitly: "nothing is certainly known" of John Greene's origins. Austin (1887) does not make this connection. The Bowridge Hill pedigree is "family tradition, not proven record." We have found no primary source linking Quidnessett John to any 1635 voyage.

    Unresolved — no primary source found

    Trap 5: The death-date conflict

    A 1685 deed implies John Greene of Quidnessett was deceased. Austin 1887, Huling 1884, and Cutter 1915 all favor c.1695-1696. Two primary-source traditions, two death dates. We do not know which is correct. We present both. This is the honest thing to do, even though it breaks the tidy narrative.

    Unresolved — source conflict documented

    Trap 6: The "proven vs probable" pressure

    Every genealogy platform wants you to click "certain" or "probable." There is no button for "we don't know." This archive is built on the radical proposition that "we don't know" is a valid and valuable research outcome. Every uncertain thing is flagged. Every conflict is preserved. The absence of evidence is treated as evidence of absence only where the record-keeping environment would predict presence.

    Operating principle — Law 1: No smoothing
    What we learned: The most dangerous thing in genealogy is not a wrong answer. It is a plausible answer that feels right. La Mance's "Joan Beggarly" feels right — it gives her a surname, a family, a place in the English world. It is also impossible. The honest path is harder: Joan/e Unknown. But it is the only path that leads to real discovery.
