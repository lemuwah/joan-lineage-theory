The Joan Archive

Restoring Access. Preserving Primary Sources. Asking Better Questions.

The Core Question

Why does a ghost of the record have to give consent?

In 1682, a deed dividing John Greene's Quidnessett lands among his sons contains a single, extraordinary clause: the transaction requires the consent of Joan, his wife. A 30-shilling life-estate is reserved to her, with a further provision — "or to her mother if she survive."

Here is the problem: Joan/e does not exist in any record before this moment. Not in baptisms. Not in marriage registers. Not in church rolls, court testimony, town meetings, estate inventories, dower claims, or gravestones. For roughly forty years — the entire documented career of one of the most litigious, paper-generating men in Narragansett Country — his wife is invisible.

And then, suddenly, English law needs her. Not as a courtesy. As a legal requirement. Where does the authority of a woman who never appeared in any prior document come from? Why does a ghost have to sign?

Places Joan/e should appear, but does not

  • Missing Birth / baptism record — No baptism found in England or New England. John Greene's own origins are disputed.
  • Missing Marriage record — No marriage record survives for John Greene to any woman, in England or Rhode Island.
  • Missing Children's baptisms — No baptismal records for Daniel, James, or any other child in Quidnessett or nearby churches.
  • Missing Church membership rolls — No Joan/e Greene in First Baptist (Providence), Newport, or any RI church records c.1640–1682.
  • Missing Town meeting minutes — No retrospective mention in Quidnessett / North Kingstown records.
  • Missing Court testimony — Women of her tier routinely appear as witnesses. Joan/e never does.
  • Missing Estate inventory / dower claim — If she survived John, she would have had dower rights. No claim appears.
  • Missing Gravestone / burial record — No gravestone, no burial record in North Kingstown or surrounding towns.
  • Missing Later genealogies (pre-1894) — Arnold, Austin, Huling, and other early RI historians do not mention her beyond "wife Joan/e."
  • Present 1682 home-place deed — Her sole appearance. The only document that names her.
The quiet is extra loud. Printing presses operated in Boston and Cambridge by the 1630s. Rhode Island courts recorded by the 1640s. Four competing colonial jurisdictions — Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, Connecticut, and Rhode Island — all generated paper. John Greene fought for his land at every turn: petitions, boundary disputes, colonial authority. He only submitted to the Crown after they guaranteed his land. A man this litigious, this documented, had a wife invisible to English law for ~40 years. That is not a research failure. That is a pattern.
The Authority Question

Where does her authority come from?

English common law in the 1680s did not require a wife's consent as a mere formality. A wife's joinder on a deed was a substantive legal act — it extinguished her dower rights, her claim to a life estate in the property. For this to happen, the wife had to be identifiable, reachable, and legally competent.

Joan/e was all three — yet none of those qualities are documented anywhere before 1682. She did not need to be "found" in 1682. She needed to be recognized. The law reached for her, and she was there. The question is not where she was hiding. The question is: what system of authority, outside the English record, made her available to English law at exactly this moment?

The "or to her mother if she survive" clause is anomalous for an English deed of this period. If Joan/e were the daughter of Alice Beggarly (d. January 1643/44), this clause would be legally meaningless — Alice had been dead for 38 years. Under Narragansett matrilineal succession, however, property rights derived from the senior matrilineal line. The clause becomes coherent: it is not an English legal formality. It is a translation of indigenous property logic into English deed language.

She Is Not Unknown

She is undigitized.

The assumption that Joan/e is "lost to history" confuses absence from databases with absence from existence. She is not unknown. She is undigitized.

Original land evidence volumes sit in the RI State Archives on microfilm. Probate files from the colonial period were never fully indexed. Church records from the 1640s–1680s exist in manuscript form, some only in copies made before fires destroyed the originals. The 1676 Newport Court Martial transcript — a document that may contain the behavioral anchor for this whole question — has not been systematically digitized.

Every "missing" record is a not-yet-requested record. The box that settles this question is probably on a shelf somewhere, not in a search engine.

🖨 Printing presses were a thing

By the 1630s, Boston and Cambridge had operating presses. Rhode Island's courts recorded by the 1640s. Four competing colonial jurisdictions generated paper. The record-keeping environment was robust — which makes Joan/e's absence statistically improbable, not expected.

🗺 Four competing territories

Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, Connecticut, and Rhode Island all claimed Narragansett Country at various points. Each generated petitions, deeds, court records, and boundary disputes. John Greene appears in all of them. Joan/e appears in none.

📦 The box is probably on a shelf

North Kingstown's records burned, but not everything. Probate files, uncatalogued deed copies, and private family papers from the 1670s–1690s may still exist in attics, historical societies, or unprocessed archival collections.

🔍 "No record found" is data

A systematic search that returns negative results narrows the field for everyone. If you check an archive and find nothing, that finding belongs in the record too. Negative space is still space.

Evidence Matrix

The four pillars of convergence

Each pillar is independently verifiable. Together, they form a circumstantial case stronger than any single document could provide.

🗺 Geographic Continuity — "Land as Witness"

The 1672 Fones Purchase deed (Anashuecot) and the 1682 home-place deed (Joan Greene) both concern land in the Quidnessett/East Greenwich corridor. John Greene is first-named purchaser in 1672; grantor in 1682. The dirt is the same. The question is whether the woman is the same.

Forensic test: Compare boundary descriptions. If "bounded by the river" or "near the path of Womphogg" appear in both deeds, the land identity strengthens the personal identity hypothesis.

⏳ Temporal Alignment — "The Presence-Absence Shift"

Anashuecot appears in 1672 — the last confirmed primary source. Joan Greene appears in 1682 — the first confirmed primary source. The 10-year gap (1672–1682) coincides with the most catastrophic period in Narragansett history: the Great Swamp Massacre (Dec 1675), Metacom's death (Aug 1676), and the mass enslavement/deportation of 500+ Narragansett.

The shift: Post-1676, surviving Narragansett individuals in colonial documents were increasingly recorded by English names rather than Narragansett names. "Anashuecot" (1672) → "Joan" (1682) is consistent with documented post-war anglicization patterns.

⚖ Behavioral Anomaly — "The Kinship Strategy"

At the 1676 Newport Court Martial, John Greene denied charges while his son Daniel testified for the English prosecution. A father-son split in the same proceeding is statistically rare. If Daniel was the child of an English mother, his testimony against his father is inexplicable. If Daniel was the child of a Narragansett mother, his testimony for the English against his father's interests becomes a survival strategy: aligning with the winning side to protect matrilineal land rights.

📜 Archival Signature — "The Produced Absence"

Joan/e's invisibility is not random. It is structured. She is absent from every English record-keeping system (church, court, probate, gravestone) and present only at the two moments when her land rights intersected English legal process: the 1672 purchase (as Anashuecot, the seller) and the 1682 division (as Joan, the wife whose consent was required). This is exactly the documentary footprint a Narragansett-English kinship union would be expected to leave.

Comparator: Sarah Stanton/Tibbitts, Mary Tibbitts/Greene, and Elizabeth Wickes all appear in church rolls, court testimony, and estate documents. Joan/e appears in none. The difference is not research failure. It is a different legal category.

The "Female Inheritance Window" (1676–1686)

Because the male sachems were dead or enslaved, the English were forced to deal with the women (Sunksquaws) to get "clean" land titles.

Joan's appearance in 1682 is not a random entry; it is a Title Confirmation. The English needed "Joan" to sign so they could legally own what "Anashuecot" had possessed. In Narragansett culture, women like Quaiapen (the Old Queen) held massive land rights. If Joan/Anashuecot was a Sunksquaw, it explains why the English had to have her signature to make their land "legal" in the eyes of the Crown.

The "Canonchet Proximity" is historically consistent: the execution of Canonchet (Nanuntenoo) in April 1676 was the most significant political event in the region. A high-status woman being "hidden" as "Joan" immediately following that event aligns with the documented "Great Migration" and "Erasure" periods of Narragansett history.

The 4 Laws

The operating principles of this archive

These four laws exist to prevent the most common errors in genealogical research: narrative smoothing, circular citation, premature exclusion, and algorithmic contamination. They are not decorative. They are enforced.

1 No Smoothing

Do not fill gaps with plausible stories. If the record is silent, say so. If two sources conflict, present both. The most dangerous thing in genealogy is not a wrong answer — it is a plausible answer that feels right. La Mance's "Joan Beggarly" feels right. It is also impossible. No smoothing means no comfort narratives.

2 La Mance Sieve

La Mance (1904) is the primary contamination vector for this family. Every claim in La Mance must be verified against primary sources before acceptance. The default assumption is error, not authority. No citation? No acceptance. Chronologically impossible? Rejected. This sieve applies to everything downstream of La Mance too — Rixford (1932), algorithmic trees, and compiled genealogies that cite him.

3 No Early Exclusion

Do not dismiss hypotheses because they are uncomfortable or unconventional. The Narragansett hypothesis for Joan/e is not proven, but it is not disproven either. It remains in the record as a working hypothesis with its evidence weighed honestly. Early exclusion is how plausible answers get buried before they are tested.

4 No Algorithmic Contamination

Ancestry, FamilySearch, MyHeritage, and YourRoots algorithmically merge profiles, propagate errors, and create circular citations. A tree with 10,000 matches is not 10,000 independent confirmations. It is one error copied 10,000 times. This archive does not accept algorithmic trees as sources. Ever.
