Key Identifications from Primary Source Spine (July 6, 2026)
Quaiapen / Matantuck — CLOSED as Anashuecot candidate
Source: Wikipedia "Quaiapen" (cross-checked against Rhode Tour, Kiddle, and Chapin's Sachems of the Narragansetts); Major John Talcott's July 4, 1676 letter.
Quaiapen (alias Magnus, Matantuck, Old Queen, Watowswokotaus) — sister of Ninigret, widow of Mixan/Mriksah (Canonicus's son) — was killed July 2, 1676 at the Second Battle of Nipsachuck. Talcott's letter names her as "Sunck squaw Magnus" slain there.
Per the project's own primary-source chain, Anashuecot was "conveyed out of ye country" as late as January 1677/8 — a year and a half after Quaiapen's death.
VERDICT: Hard chronological elimination. Quaiapen cannot be Anashuecot. This is the primary stated reason, with territorial-overlap and title parallels demoted to supporting context only.
Stonewall John — Smith-orbit connection CONFIRMED
Source: Wikipedia "Stonewall John" (quoting Roger Williams letter, April 1, 1676); Smith's Castle RI Slave History Medallion essay.
Primary quote, contemporary: the April 1, 1676 letter describes Stonewall John as "Mr R.Smiths John Wall Maker" — Smith's own name attached to him in period text. Smith's Castle's institutional essay notes Williams' correspondence calls Stonewall John "Richard Smith's man" — notably NOT the term "servant" Williams used for his own slaves.
This confirms and strengthens the existing note that Stonewall John was "a former Richard Smith trading-post employee" — now anchored to a primary quotation, not just a secondary characterization.
Valentine Whitman / Wightman — NOT YET DISAMBIGUATED
Source: Smith's Castle RI Slave History Medallion essay; Sakonnet.net "Richard Smith" page; grindalshoalsgazette.com.
Two spellings, two possible identities:
"Valentine Whitman" — early interpreter for Smith at his trading post, called "Smith's man" by Williams (same non-servant designation as Stonewall John).
"Valentine Wightman (1627-1700)" — fluent in Algonquian, known to Williams, Smith, and Winthrop; sold his brother George Wightman a 100-acre Narragansett Bay-frontage plot called "Aquidnesset" (i.e. Quidnessett) in his will.
FLAG: "Whitman" and "Wightman" are plausible period-spelling variants. Same forename, same generation, same locale, same three named contacts. This LOOKS like one person described two ways — but has not been verified as such. Treat as PROBABLE, not confirmed, pending primary-source check.
WHY THIS MATTERS: If the interpreter Valentine and the Quidnessett-landholding Valentine are the same man, then a second, independent "Quidnessett" landholding existed in the same neighborhood as John Greene of Quidnessett's own homestead — raising a Quidnessett-place-name hazard structurally identical to the Four-John and three-Joans firewalls already in place.
VERDICT: Pseudonym hypothesis tested and rejected — Valentine b.1627 England, arrived America c.1648, nine to eleven years after John Greene is already documented at Cocumscussoc/Quidnessett (c.1637-39). No chronological window for overlap.
Quidnessett boundary question: CLOSED — neighboring, not identical tracts. George Wightman's land neighbors "John Greene Sr. and Jr." on the 1717/18 map. Separate, adjacent, individually-mapped tracts. Greene and Wightman were literal neighbors at Quidnessett.
Four-John Firewall — Bartlett Vol.III primary text obtained
Source: Records of the Colony of Rhode Island, Vol. III (Bartlett), pp. multiple.
April 1678 roster: "Capt'n John Green" listed as Assistant (colony-wide magistrate). Same session, separately: "Mr. John Greene" is Deputy for Newport; "Mr. John Greene, engaged" is Conservator of the Peace for Kings Towne.
May 1679 roster: "Lieut. John Greene" appears as Deputy for Newport. "John Greene, Jun'r" admitted as freeman of Warwick — a distinct, junior line.
REFINED HYPOTHESIS: Three separate Greene role-lines in this single window:
"Capt'n John Green[e]" — Assistant (1678), London envoy/signatory. Military rank + colony-wide elected office. Fits Deputy Gov. John Greene Jr. of Warwick better than Quidnessett John.
"Mr. John Greene, engaged" — Conservator of the Peace, KINGS TOWNE (1678). Plain "Mr." title, local office, geographically sited exactly where Quidnessett John lived. Very likely Quidnessett John himself.
"Mr. John Greene" / "Lieut. John Greene" — Deputy for NEWPORT (1678, 1679). A third, separate seat. Possibly connects to Bates's subject, "John Greene of Newport" (the Abigail Wardwell husband, the "Fifth John").
NOT YET CONFIRMED: Whether #1 and #2 could be one man holding two offices (unusual but not impossible), or whether #2 and #3 could be connected across a later move Newport-ward.

"His marke" question — CONSOLIDATED VERDICT
Cross-tool convergence (Kimi audit):
Three confirmed real documents showing accurate "her mark" / "his mark" gender-tracking:
Sonksqua deed — female sachem correctly uses "her mark"
Jane Sears, 1640 Providence Combination — "Jane Sears X her mark (widow)" alongside men's "his mark"
Joan Greene's own 1682 deed — correctly uses "her mark"
ZERO confirmed counterexamples of a woman signing with "his mark" in 17th-century New England found by either research effort.
HOWEVER: Absence of a counterexample is not proof of accuracy in this specific instance. This remains a genuinely open contradiction, to be held per the Contradiction Preservation Law.
NEW LINE OF ARGUMENT: Algonquian languages (including Narragansett) have NO masculine/feminine grammatical gender. Their noun/pronoun system distinguishes animate vs. inanimate only. If the 1672 transaction was negotiated in Narragansett, the scribe recording it in English had no gendered pronoun to translate from in the source language at all. "His" in the deed cannot be a faithful rendering of a gendered original — there was nothing gendered to render. The pronoun necessarily came from an external source: personal knowledge, an interpreter's report, or an assumption riding on the English default that a "chief sachem" (an authority position) reads as male absent contrary information.
VERDICT: This does NOT prove Anashuecot was female. It DOES shift the evidentiary weight of "his marke" — from a linguistically-encoded fact faithfully carried across translation, to one scribe's added claim or assumption of unknown origin and unknown reliability.
Convergence-independence test — NEGATIVE result
Per hostile-review Item 4:
Tested whether secondary "convergence" sources (alfredgibbs.com, WikiTree, Geni, Findagrave, Painted Hills/Maxson Greene, etc.) are genuinely independent lines of evidence or share common upstream origins.
Findings: alfredgibbs.com's deep English pedigree is sourced to an unnamed "the book," which the site itself describes as "family tradition...not a fully documented chain in the modern sense," with acknowledged gaps. WikiTree (Greene-950) explicitly states La Mance (1903/04) and Rixford are "Fabricated Pedigree...SHOULD NOT BE USED."
VERDICT: Convergence independence is NOT established. Multiple sources trace to the same contested, partially-discredited compilations (La Mance, Rixford, and to a lesser extent Austin 1887), not separate evidentiary lines. This needs disclosure in any future packet version.
