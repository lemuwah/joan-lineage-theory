## 1. Starting Question

Does "Coginiquant" (Potter 1835, pp. 56–59 — lessee to the Smiths at Cocumscussoc, March 8, 1656 and June 8, 1659), "Cononjonant" (Brooks, *Our Beloved Kin* — "cousin to her late husband" Mixanno, the man reportedly gotten drunk to sign the June 11, 1659 Atherton Purchase deed), and "Cojonoquond" (Potter 1835, pp. 98/173 via Native Northeast Portal citation — nephew of Miantonomo, father of Quanopen/Quinnapin) name one person, two people, or three — and specifically, whose name is actually on the June 11, 1659 instrument?

This matters for the wider project because the answer determines whether Quaiapen's land authority was transferred through a kinsman of her own late husband's line (a controlled, internally logical succession event) or through an unrelated senior-line figure (which would reopen questions about who actually held decision rights over Cocumscussoc in 1659) — and because the "gotten drunk to sign" detail, if it belongs to the wrong name, could currently be misattributed in the project's own synthesis.

## 2. Hypotheses

### H-CG1: Single Individual — Transcription Drift Across Three Sources
**Claim:** All three names refer to one person. Potter's 1656/1659 "Coginiquant" (lessee), Brooks's "Cononjonant" (1659 deed signer, Mixanno's cousin), and the Potter pp.98/173 "Cojonoquond" (Miantonomo's nephew, Quanopen's father) are the same man passing through three transcription layers — Potter's own 19th-century antiquarian hand, a 20th-century secondary paraphrase (Brooks), and a modern portal's re-citation of Potter. The lessee of Cocumscussoc land in 1656/59 would then plausibly be the same person authorized (or coerced) to sign away that land three days later.

**Where evidence might hide in plain sight:** In Potter 1835 itself, pp. 56–60 — if Potter used a *consistent* spelling for one referent across both the lease entries and the Atherton narrative, the "drift" would only be Brooks's later paraphrase, not Potter's. Checking whether Potter spells the lessee and the signer identically settles half the question without leaving the source.

**What boring document might quietly reflect this:** Any 1650s Narragansett kinship list or genealogy appendix (e.g., in Arnold 1894 or a modern ethnohistorical genealogy of the Canonicus–Miantonomo–Pessacus line) that places a single individual in both a "nephew of Miantonomo" slot and a "cousin of Mixanno" slot — the two kin-terms are not mutually exclusive in Narragansett reckoning and could describe one man from two different relational vantage points.

**Pattern that would support it:** If the same name-bearer appears leasing Cocumscussoc land (1656, 1659) and also appears as father of Quanopen in a source independent of Potter (e.g., a separate deed or deposition naming Quanopen's father directly), that convergence would be hard to explain as coincidence.

**Contradiction if false:** Potter himself uses two visibly different name-forms for the lessee versus the pp.98/173 figure, with no editorial note treating them as the same person; or an independent source dates Cojonoquond's death, birth, or activity to a period inconsistent with signing in 1659.

**Testability:** HIGH — resolved by direct re-reading of Potter 1835 pp. 56–60 and pp. 98/173 side by side for internal consistency.

### H-CG2: Two Individuals — Lessee/Signer Are One; Miantonomo's Nephew Is Distinct
**Claim:** "Coginiquant" (lessee, 1656/59) and "Cononjonant" (signer, June 11 1659) are the same man — a Mixanno-line cousin with direct standing to lease and later sign away the same tract. "Cojonoquond" (Miantonomo's nephew) is a different, senior-line person who happens to share a phonetically adjacent name in a small, name-recycling onomastic pool.

**Where evidence might hide in plain sight:** In the *dates* alone. If the lease entries (1656, 1659) and the Atherton signing (June 11, 1659) sit only three days apart and involve the same tract, the internal logic of "same lessee signs the same land away" is strong on its face — this hypothesis requires less reconstruction than H-CG1, which also has to prove the Miantonomo-nephew connection.

**What boring document might quietly reflect this:** Probate or land-dispute records from the 1660s–1680s in which Quanopen (Cojonoquond's alleged son) asserts inherited rights to land *other than* Cocumscussoc — if Quanopen's documented land interests lie outside the Cocumscussoc/Atherton tract entirely, that weakens any link between his father and the June 11 deed.

**Pattern that would support it:** Cocumscussoc-specific documents (leases, the Atherton deed, later Smith-family land disputes) cluster around one name-form, while Miantonomo/Quanopen-lineage documents cluster around a separate, non-overlapping set of place names.

**Contradiction if false:** A single document ties Cojonoquond explicitly to the Cocumscussoc lease or the Atherton signing by name, place, and date together (not just phonetic resemblance).

**Testability:** MEDIUM — requires a Quanopen land-interest survey independent of Potter.

### H-CG3: Three Distinct Individuals — Coincidental Phonetic Convergence
**Claim:** The apparent cluster is an artifact of a small 17th-century Narragansett name-pool being transcribed by English scribes with limited phonetic vocabulary for Algonquian sounds. Three separate men — a Cocumscussoc lessee, a Mixanno-line cousin, and a Canonicus/Miantonomo-line nephew — happen to produce name-forms that look alike to modern readers but were not confused by contemporaries.

**Where evidence might hide in plain sight:** In any *single* period document that names two of the three roles (lessee, Atherton signer, Miantonomo's nephew) as clearly different people in the same sentence or the same page — an incidental disambiguation that neither Potter nor Brooks was trying to make, but that a scribe made naturally because he knew both men.

**What boring document might quietly reflect this:** Witness lists on unrelated 1650s–1660s Narragansett-country deeds (land sales having nothing to do with Cocumscussoc) that include more than one of these name-forms as separate signatories on the same instrument.

**Pattern that would support it:** The name-forms, when plotted against known Narragansett naming conventions (patronymic elements, totemic elements, title elements), resolve into three linguistically distinct roots rather than one root under three spellings.

**Contradiction if false:** No period document ever lists two of the name-forms as co-present, distinct people — the total silence on differentiation, across dozens of Narragansett-country deeds naming other individuals precisely, would itself be evidence against three real, socially prominent, separate men.

**Testability:** MEDIUM-LOW — requires a comparative phonetic/etymological pass across all three forms against attested Narragansett name elements, which is interpretive rather than purely archival.

### H-CG4: Title or Kin-Term Recorded as a Proper Name
**Claim:** "Cononjonant" is not a personal name at all but an English scribal rendering of a Narragansett kinship or status term ("cousin," "in-law," or a specific affinal category tied to succession) that got copied by Brooks as if it were the man's name. The actual personal name of the June 11, 1659 signer is one of the other forms (most likely Coginiquant), and "Cononjonant" is a mistranslated relational label sitting adjacent to the real name in the source Brooks drew from.

**Where evidence might hide in plain sight:** In Brooks's own footnotes/citations in *Our Beloved Kin* for the "cousin to her late husband" passage — if Brooks is quoting or paraphrasing a specific colonial-era document, that document's exact wording (not Brooks's paraphrase) may show a kin-term followed by a separate proper name, both of which got compressed into "Cononjonant" in the secondary literature.

**What boring document might quietly reflect this:** Roger Williams's *Key into the Language of America*, which catalogs kinship terminology — if a term resembling "Cononjonant" appears there as a relational word rather than a name, that would directly support this hypothesis.

**Pattern that would support it:** Other colonial documents using the same or a similar term as a descriptor attached to different individuals across different deeds — i.e., the "word" recurs with different proper names beside it, rather than always referring to the same man.

**Contradiction if false:** "Cononjonant" appears in a colonial document as a standalone signature or is referred to elsewhere with possessives or third-person continuity ("Cononjonant's land," "the said Cononjonant") in a way only a proper name would be used.

**Testability:** MEDIUM — requires checking Williams's *Key* and Brooks's underlying citation trail, both plausibly accessible without archive travel.

---

### H-CG5: The Question Resolves Entirely at the Primary Layer — Modern Secondary Sources Are the Actual Source of Confusion
**Claim:** Potter 1835 and Arnold 1894, read directly against each other, already name the June 11, 1659 signer unambiguously and consistently. The apparent three-way ambiguity is manufactured entirely by later secondary/tertiary layering — Brooks's popular synthesis and the Native Northeast Portal's re-citation of Potter — neither of which the project has yet checked against Potter's and Arnold's actual page images rather than paraphrase.

**Where evidence might hide in plain sight:** Nowhere subtle at all — this is the "just go read pp. 58–60" hypothesis. If it's true, the resolution requires no inference, only retrieval.

**What boring document might quietly reflect this:** Arnold's 1894 index entry itself — a 19th-century index compiled independently of Potter, cross-referencing the same 1659 deed, is exactly the kind of unglamorous document that could settle the question in one line and has been sitting in the outstanding-targets list without being pulled.

**Pattern that would support it:** Potter and Arnold agree with each other and disagree with Brooks — the disagreement being explainable by Brooks working from a modern secondary paraphrase rather than the primary deed language.

**Contradiction if false:** Potter and Arnold themselves disagree with each other on the signer's name — in which case the ambiguity is genuinely at the primary-source level and none of H-CG1–H-CG4 can be resolved by document retrieval alone; the project would then need onomastic/interpretive analysis regardless.

**Testability:** HIGH — this is the cheapest hypothesis to test and should be attempted first, since a positive result collapses the entire open thread in one step.
